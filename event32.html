Class, Labor, and Political Economy

1821 – Lowell Mills

The Lowell Mills were textile mills in Lowell, Massachusetts, established in 1821 and named after Francis Cabot Lowell, that centralized the textile manufacturing process under one roof. These mills were operated by the Merrimack River in northern Massachusetts and by local farm girls known as “mill girls.” The concerning working conditions and low wages that laborers in mills faced sparked hundreds of labor strikes to demand reform, and women were a dominant force in this. Unions like the Female Labor Reform Association, led by Sarah Badgley, a female operative in one of the Lowell Mills, organized petition drives that earned thousands of signatures from mill girls. Despite this, the Lowell Mills brought a rush of capital and entrepreneurs into New England and established the modern American factory.
