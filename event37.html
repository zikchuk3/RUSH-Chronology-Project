1920 – 19th Amendment

The 19th Amendment was ratified to the Constitution on August 26, 1920, and granted American women the right to vote. The passage of the 19th Amendment was the outcome of years of advocacy for women’s rights, dating as far back as 1948, when the first women’s rights convention, the Seneca Falls Convention, took place. In that convention, Elizabeth Cady Stanton delivered the “Declaration of Sentiments,” which demanded women’s suffrage and that women be treated as equal to men. The passage of the 19th Amendment not only gave women more political and civil rights, but also challenged traditional gender roles, setting the stage for more years of advocacy for women and the Women’s Liberation Movement.
